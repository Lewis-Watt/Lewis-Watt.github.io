Overview

After graduating with a First Class Masters degree in Physics with Astronomy from the University of Nottingham, I undertook a PhD project at the University of Bristol which I completed in March 2023. My PhD project has me working alongside an ever growing and diverse Planetary Formation and Atmospheres group, developing software in Python and C++ to simulate and analyse late-stage planetary formation. As part of the PhD, I have had the opportunity to present my work and network at international conferences, lead large classrooms of undergraduate students, and work with international partners to publish research which all show my skills in presenting, leadership, and teamwork.

My research interests lie in: Planet formation, planetesimal formation, numerical modeling (giant impacts Gadget2 + debris disk own N-body code/Rebound), debris disk evolution, planetary evolution, and exoplanets.

Skills

An example video is shown below. This involved understanding how to integrate the movement of particles overtime which were affected by gravity of large bodies in the system. I used a leapfrog integrator which had multistepping functionality. The multistepping allowed particles to drop down to smaller timesteps to increase precision when close to a gravitating body. I have been able to present my work at international conferences as part of contributed talks. This allowed me to build up a network of people with interests in my area of research. I also collaborated with people based abroad to better understand the observational side of extreme debris disks.

Research

My research currently focuses on how we can observe the giant impact phase of terrestrial planet formation. Extreme debris disks are a smoking gun for a recent impact with the brightness and variation in their lightcurves not behaving like a traditional debris disk should. My have done numerical studies using SPH, N-body and radiative transfer codes to show how the variation seen in extrmee debris disk lightcurves can be caused by a recent giant impact. The work mostly uses a combination of C/C++ and Python code.

I currently have two main authored (one which is in the review process) publications and was involved in a supervision role on a project which started as an extension to my work. These works are summarised below.

Wiggly Extreme Debris Disks

Some extreme debris disks show periodicity in their near infrared lightcurves on orbital timescales (e.g. ID8). The periodicity in the lightcurve along with a longer trend with the disk brightening/dimming has been identified to be potentially caused by dust escaping a recent giant impact between two planetary embryos. The escaping dust forms a circumstellar disk around the star leading to an increasd excess in the infrared light observed from the system. Planetary Embryos are a good candidates for recent impact induced disks as they are likely to impact at speeds needed to vapourise mantle material. The vapourisation of mantle material is key to osbserving impact induced disks as vapourised mantle will form condisates into small size distribution centred on mm/cm sized grains on timescales needed to be observed one to two orbits after the impact has occurred.

In Watt et al., (2021) we wanted to better understand the effect of different giant impacts had on the formation of extreme derbis disks. To do so we conducted a three part numerical study which took us from the initial giant impact to the estimated lightcurve. This involved SPH simulations of giant impacts in GADGET-2, N-body simulations of the escaping dust population and radiative transfer calculations done with RADMC3D. Below we show an example of a giant impact between two equal-sized planetary embryos.

One key finding I discovered was that all giant impacts will distribute escaping dust anisotropically. The anisotropic distribution has a massive impact on the disk morphology depending on the orientation of the giant impact with respect to the position around the central star. Below I show the effect of changing the orientation can have on the morphology of a disk created from post-impact ejecta and how the infrared excess can change as a respect to this.

For a head on impact above we can see that the orientation can change the morphology of the disk greatly. This is then reflected in the excess flux we observe from the disk with the blue disk showing the dips in the lightcurve we would expect while we do not see the dips in the orange lightcurve. This could potentially have a huge impact on what we would expect to observe from giant-impact induced disks since orientation of the impact will matter greatly. For greater details please see the published work in Watt et al., (2021).

Planetesimals sustaining Extreme Debris Disks

If an extreme debris disk is only made up of small dust grains, then one would expect the collisional cascade between dust grains to grind down the grain sizes to below blow-out size of the star and lost from the system on extremely quick timescales. We often find the excess flux from extreme debris disks to be at an elevated level for years which does not fit the quick processing picture. One way to solve this conundrum is to have a population of material which is larger in size grind down over time which replenishes the dust grains lost.

The escaping material post-giant impact is not only vapourised but is a mix of melted and vapourised material. There is potential for the melted material that is escaping the giant impact to form large objects known as planetesimals (1-1000 km in diameter) as long as the formation process is not impeded by the expanding vapour. In my giant impact simulations I have indentified a planetesimal population that should be collisionally active in the post-impact circumstellar disk soon after the giant impact has occurred. Using REBOUND, I have simulated a disk of collisionally active planetesimals to show how the most destructive collisions between planetesimals can add material on timescales short enough to sustain the observed extreme debris disk. Below I show the timescales we expect disks fromed from one giant impact and the subsequent planetesimal collisions to be observable for. More details will soon be published.

Dust Clump Transits

There is potential for a giant impact to occur and form a disk which is observed (near) edge-on. The initial dust clump post-giant impact has the potential to transit in front of the star producing transit lightcurves in optical wavelengths which are unlike any planetary transits we have seen! Some extreme debris disks have been observed to have strange transit-like events before and during an increase in their excess flux. Below I show an example of a dust clump transiting in front of a star from my simulations. This work is currently ongoing.
